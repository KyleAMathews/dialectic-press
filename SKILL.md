# Sloptractions Synthesis

Transform the raw outputs of a Hegelian dialectic into a polished essay in the style of Venkatesh Rao's "sloptractions" — AI-assisted writing that operates at "meso" abstraction (more detail than humans can hold unassisted, less than AI can handle autonomously), with a structural recipe at the end that exposes how the piece was made.

## When to Use This Skill

Use when:
- You've completed a dialectic (or at least one full round) and want to turn it into a shareable essay
- The dialectic produced genuine insight — a reframing, a hidden question, a synthesis that transforms the original tension
- You want the essay to be **pleasurable, not just summarizable** — the reader should want to read the actual text, not just get the gist
- You want to expose the method (the recipe) so readers can apply similar moves to their own thinking

Do NOT use when:
- The dialectic didn't produce real insight (polish won't fix shallow thinking)
- You want a standard blog post or summary (use normal writing)
- The material is purely technical/procedural (sloptractions style is for conceptual territory)

## The Sloptractions Method

Based on analysis of Rao's sloptractions corpus (LLMs as Index Funds, Prompting is Managing, Autoamputation Flow, History of Memeing, etc.), the method has these distinguishing features:

### Core Structural Pattern

1. **Provocation/Hook** — A concrete phenomenon that seems to illustrate something larger (baby names diversifying, an EEG study on LLM-assisted writing, GameStop meme stocks)
2. **Reframing via analogy** — Map the phenomenon onto a well-understood system from another domain (index funds, management, hyperstition)
3. **Conceptual currency** — Coin or adopt precise terms (linguistic alpha/beta, prompting-managing equivalence, autoamputation flow)
4. **Structural unpacking** — Decompose into layers, phases, or tensions (the layered AI stack, two-tier linguistic economy, four routes through reality)
5. **Implications/Stakes** — What follows if the reframing is correct?
6. **Recipe** — Expose the generative moves as imperative verbs

### What Makes This Different from Normal Writing

- **Meso-level detail density** — holds many threads coherently; would be exhausting to produce manually
- **Structural transparency** — the recipe says "here's how the sausage was made"
- **The human brings the moves** — reframe, unpack, trace propagation, introduce hidden variable, coin term
- **The AI executes at density** — fills out implications, generates prose that holds many threads

### Recipe Variations (from Rao's corpus)

| Style | When to Use | Example |
|-------|-------------|---------|
| **Numbered conceptual moves** | When the essay follows a clear build-up sequence | "1. Start with structural analogy. 2. Establish historical precedent. 3. Define conceptual currency..." |
| **Process description** | When the work was more exploratory | "scan → reframe → structure → style" |
| **Co-authoring protocol** | When you want to expose the human-AI collaboration | "Thematic seeding → Structural design → Section-by-section drafting → Poetic compression..." |
| **Honest mess** | When the process was genuinely messy | "Cut-and-paste synthesis from multiple iterations. Each Deep Research run functioned like a new first draft..." |

## Phase 1: Inventory Dialectic Outputs

Before writing, inventory what you have from the dialectic. You'll draw from all of these:

| Dialectic Output | Sloptractions Use |
|------------------|-------------------|
| **Context briefing** | Background for the hook; source of concrete phenomena |
| **The original contradiction** | The provocation — the tension readers will recognize |
| **Monk essays** | Steelmanned positions to present and then transcend; source of evidence and examples |
| **Determinate negation** | The specific failures that point toward what's missing — this is often the most interesting material |
| **Hidden question** | The reframing that transforms the debate |
| **Synthesis** | The new concept — what gets cancelled/preserved/elevated |
| **Misfits/queue** | Honest limitations; open loops for the conclusion |
| **The dialectic process itself** | Raw material for the recipe |

Read through these files and identify:
- **The hook** — what concrete phenomenon will draw readers in?
- **The surprising turn** — what did the dialectic reveal that wasn't obvious at the start?
- **The new concept** — what term or framework emerges from the synthesis?
- **The honest limits** — what tensions remain unresolved?

---

## Phase 2: Find the Analogical Domain

The dialectic operates *within* a domain (web frameworks, fat loss methods, institutional identity). The sloptractions essay needs to *map that domain onto another* to create leverage.

Ask: What well-understood system does this dialectic's structure resemble?

| Dialectic Domain | Possible Analogical Domains |
|------------------|----------------------------|
| Technology choices | Financial instruments (index funds vs. active management), ecological niches, evolutionary arms races |
| Organizational design | Biological systems, game theory, thermodynamics |
| Personal decisions | Navigation/wayfinding, gardening, investment |
| Creative work | Sculpture (removal), cooking (combination), architecture |
| Epistemic questions | Legal reasoning, scientific method, debugging |

**The best analogies are structural, not superficial.** "LLMs are like index funds" works because both exhibit: convergence to mean, suppression of variance, cheap exposure to average, alpha/beta dynamics. The analogy does conceptual work, not just decorative work.

---

## Phase 3: Coin Conceptual Currency

From the dialectic's synthesis and determinate negation, extract or coin terms that will do load-bearing work in the essay.

Good conceptual currency:
- **Names a real distinction** the reader can now see and use (linguistic alpha/beta, chimeric AI-self)
- **Compresses a complex dynamic** into a handle (autoamputation flow, hyperstition)
- **Reframes both sides** of the original tension (prompting-managing equivalence)

Ask yourself:
- What's the key distinction the synthesis revealed?
- What would you call the dynamic that both sides were missing?
- What metaphor from the analogical domain applies?

Write down 2-4 candidate terms. Test each: "Can I use this to think about other cases? Does it do work, or is it just a label?"

## Phase 4: Outline the Arc

Sloptractions essays follow a recognizable arc. Map your dialectic material onto it:

```
1. HOOK (300-500 words)
   - Concrete phenomenon readers will recognize
   - Brief statement of the apparent tension
   - Signal that a reframing is coming

2. THE OBVIOUS FRAMING (300-500 words)
   - Present the two sides as most people see them
   - (This is compressed from your monk essays)
   - Make each side genuinely compelling

3. THE TURN (500-800 words)
   - Introduce the analogical domain
   - Show how the analogy reframes the tension
   - Deploy your conceptual currency
   - This is where the hidden question emerges

4. STRUCTURAL UNPACKING (800-1500 words)
   - Decompose into layers/phases/tensions
   - Use evidence from monk essays + context briefing
   - Trace implications through the structure
   - This is meso-level territory — more detail than a hot take, less than a treatise

5. STAKES & IMPLICATIONS (300-500 words)
   - What follows if the reframing is correct?
   - What should readers do/think differently?
   - Name the open questions (from misfits/queue)

6. RECIPE (200-400 words)
   - Expose the generative moves
   - Use imperative verbs: "Start with...", "Reframe...", "Identify...", "Trace..."
   - Make it usable — someone should be able to apply this to a different topic
```

**Adjust proportions based on material.** If the structural unpacking is especially rich (a 4-layer model, a 7-phase process), give it more space. If the hook is viscerally compelling, linger there.

## Phase 5: Write the Draft

Now write, using the dialectic outputs as raw material. Some principles:

### Voice & Tone
- **Analytical but alive** — not academic, not casual
- **Confident claims** — "The real difference is..." not "One might argue that..."
- **Let the ideas carry the charge** — don't amp up with rhetoric

### Integrating Dialectic Material
- **Don't reproduce monk essays** — compress them to their strongest single-paragraph form
- **Quote sparingly** — use the best formulations, not everything
- **The determinate negation is gold** — the specific failures are often the most interesting sentences in the piece
- **The synthesis should feel like arrival** — readers should think "oh, *that's* what this is really about"

### Meso-Level Density
- **Hold many threads** — reference earlier points, connect across sections
- **Be specific** — names, mechanisms, examples, not just abstractions
- **But don't drown** — if a paragraph has 5+ distinct claims, break it up

### The Recipe
Write the recipe *as you write*, not after. Note the moves you're making. This keeps you honest about the structure and produces a better recipe.

---

## Phase 6: Validate & Polish

Before finalizing:

1. **Does the analogy do work?** If you removed it, would the essay collapse or survive? (It should need the analogy.)

2. **Is the synthesis genuinely new?** Or is it "both sides have merits" dressed up? (If the latter, go back to the dialectic.)

3. **Can a reader use the conceptual currency?** Try: "In situation X, is this 'alpha' or 'beta'?" If the distinction doesn't help, the term isn't doing work.

4. **Is the recipe usable?** Try applying it to a different topic in your head. If it's too specific to this essay, generalize it.

5. **Is it pleasurable to read?** Read it aloud. If you find yourself skimming, tighten or cut.

## Example: Fat Loss Methods Dialectic → Sloptractions Essay

**Dialectic outputs:**
- Context briefing on fat loss research (calories, hormones, behavior, etc.)
- Monk A: "Calories are the only thing that matters — thermodynamics is physics"
- Monk B: "Hormones and food quality determine fat storage — it's biochemistry, not accounting"
- Determinate negation: Both assume the individual body is the unit of analysis; both miss the environmental/behavioral frame
- Synthesis: "Fat loss is an emergent property of sustainable behavior patterns, not a direct consequence of any single mechanism"

**Sloptractions transformation:**

1. **Hook:** The concrete phenomenon of why people who count calories obsessively often regain weight (surprising given their physics-is-physics claim)

2. **Analogical domain:** Control systems engineering — thermostats, feedback loops, setpoints

3. **Conceptual currency:** 
   - "Caloric accounting" vs. "metabolic sensing" (the two sides)
   - "Behavioral sustainability gradient" (the actual determinant)
   - "Regime stability" (what actually matters for long-term outcomes)

4. **The turn:** Both sides are describing *mechanisms* when the actual question is about *system stability*. A thermostat doesn't care whether you heat the room with gas or electric — it cares whether the system reaches and maintains the setpoint. Fat loss is the same: the question isn't which mechanism is correct, but which regime is stable for this person in this environment.

5. **Recipe:**
   - Start with a paradox that believers in Position A can't explain
   - Map the domain onto control systems
   - Show both positions as describing mechanisms, not stability
   - Introduce the "regime stability" frame
   - Trace implications for what advice actually helps
   - Name the open question: can you engineer regime stability, or is it mostly environmental luck?

## Output

The final essay should be saved as a markdown file alongside the dialectic outputs. Include:

1. **The essay itself** (2000-4000 words typically)
2. **The recipe section** (explicitly marked, at the end)
3. **Optional: Author's note** on the dialectic-to-essay process (useful for your own learning)

Name the file clearly: `essay_[topic].md` or similar.

---

## Relationship to the Dialectic Skill

This skill is a **downstream transformation** of dialectic output. The dialectic produces understanding; this skill produces communication.

The quality ceiling of the essay is set by the quality of the dialectic. If the synthesis was shallow, the essay will be shallow. If the determinate negation was vague, you won't have the specific failures that make the "turn" work.

If you find the essay isn't coming together, the problem is usually upstream — return to the dialectic and push further.
