# Phase 3: Coin Conceptual Currency

## Goal

Extract or coin terms that will do load-bearing work in the essay. Good conceptual currency lets readers think with your ideas after they finish reading.

## What Makes Good Conceptual Currency

From Rao's corpus:

| Term | What It Does |
|------|--------------|
| **Linguistic alpha/beta** | Names a real distinction (original vs. average-case language) |
| **Autoamputation flow** | Compresses a complex dynamic into a handle |
| **Prompting-managing equivalence** | Reframes both sides of the original tension |
| **Chimeric AI-self** | Makes a hidden thing visible |

Good terms:
- **Name a real distinction** the reader can now see and use
- **Compress a complex dynamic** into a handle
- **Reframe both sides** of the original tension
- **Import from the analogical domain** where useful

Bad terms:
- Just labels for things that already have names
- Too abstract to apply ("holistic synthesis")
- Too specific to this one case

## Sources for Terms

Draw from:

1. **The analogical domain** (Phase 2)
   - What vocabulary comes with it?
   - What would the dialectic's elements be called in that domain?

2. **The synthesis**
   - What got "elevated"? Does it have a name?
   - What's the new unit of analysis?

3. **The determinate negation**
   - What's the shared assumption both sides missed?
   - What's the specific failure mode each exhibited?

4. **Unexpected combinations**
   - Compound terms that juxtapose domains (linguistic + alpha)
   - Verbs turned into nouns (autoamputation → autoamputation flow)

## Generate Candidates

For each key concept in your inventory (Phase 1), generate 2-3 candidate terms.

Test each candidate:
- **Can I use this to think about other cases?** Try applying it to a situation outside this dialectic.
- **Does it do work, or is it just a label?** Would removing the term weaken the argument?
- **Is it memorable?** Can you imagine someone using it in conversation?

## The Minimum Set

You need at least:
1. **A term for the reframing** — what the essay reveals (e.g., "prompting-managing equivalence")
2. **A term for the old binary** (optional but useful) — what readers thought the debate was about
3. **A term for the key dynamic** — the mechanism or process the synthesis identified

More is fine, but don't coin terms for the sake of coining. Every term should earn its place.

---

## Checkpoint

Present candidate terms to the user:

> "Here are the conceptual terms I'm proposing for the essay:
> 
> **For the reframing:**
> - [Term A]: [what it means, why it works]
> - [Term B]: [alternative, if you have one]
> 
> **For the key dynamic:**
> - [Term]: [what it captures]
> 
> **From the analogical domain:**
> - [Imported term]: [how it applies here]
> 
> **Optional/secondary:**
> - [Any other terms worth considering]
> 
> Which of these do work? Any that feel like empty labels? Terms you'd prefer instead?"

**Wait for user validation before proceeding to Phase 4.**

The user may:
- Approve the terms
- Reject terms that don't resonate
- Propose alternatives
- Ask you to generate more options

Iterate until you have a validated term set.
